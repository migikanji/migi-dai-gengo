What is kanji?

Kanji is one of the writing systems in Japanese. What makes it different from katakana and hiragana is that kanji consists of ideograms, while kana are phonetic alphabets. But why do we need to learn kanji? Well, kanji came from Chinese and became a fundamental part of the Japanese language. These characters were created to represent meanings and make writing more efficient.

The history of kanji

Kanji are ideograms, but what does that mean? Ideograms are characters that represent an idea or meaning rather than a sound. Many ideograms originally came from drawings, for example: 山 (yama, mountain) → Came from a drawing of a mountain. 人 (hito, person) → Came from the silhouette of a walking person. 川 (kawa, river) → Is based on the shape of a flowing river. 大 (dai, big) → Represents a person with arms extended to show a large size. These ideograms evolved over time into the kanji we use today and Kanji comes from Chinese, but Japan has unique kanji called kokuji (国字). What are kokuji? They are kanji created in Japan, not borrowed from Chinese. Some examples are 鰯, 鰰, and others. There are also different types of kanji depending on how they evolved: Traditional Chinese: 氣 Simplified Chinese: 气 Simplified Japanese: 気 As you can see, there are many variations of kanji.

Where do kanji readings come from?

Kanji have two types of readings: Onyomi (音読み): The reading of Chinese origin. Kunyomi (訓読み): The reading of Japanese origin. The onyomi will be written in katakana, while the kunyomi will be written in hiragana. For example, with the kanji 山 (mountain): Onyomi: サン (san, adapted from Chinese "shān") Kunyomi: やま (yama, native Japanese word) Another example with the kanji 水 (water): Onyomi: スイ (sui, from Chinese "shuǐ") Kunyomi: みず (mizu, native Japanese word) This duality exists because Japanese adopted kanji from Chinese while keeping their own native words.
